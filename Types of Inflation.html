Types of Inflation

There are six types of inflation. They are:

  • Demand-pull inflation
  • Cost-push inflation
  • Wage Push Inflation
  • Imported Inflation
  • Core Inflation
  • Hyper Inflation

Demand-pull inflation

Demand-pull inflation normally happens when the econormy is expanding more quickly than the long-term trend pace of expansion. Businesses will raise prices if demand outpaces supply.

Cost-push inflation

This occurs when firms' cost of production rise, causing the aggregate supply to shift to the left. Producing energy and commodity prices may be the source of cost-push inflation.

Diagram showing cost-push inflation

Wage Push Inflation

Inflation is often caused by rising salaries. This is essentially a mix of cost-push and demand-pull inflation. Businesses incur increased costs as a result of rising salaries, which are then passed on to customers in the form of higher pricing.

Imported Inflation

The cost of imports will increase if the exchange rate declines. As a result, this exchange rate effect alone will cause the prices to rise. Additionally, a depreciation will boost demand by making exports more competitive.

Core Inflation

Core inflation is one way to measure inflation. This is the rate of inflation that does not include short-term "volatile" components like the cost of food and energy.

Hyperinflation (> 1000%)

There is no precise definition, but it is reserved for extreme forms of inflation, typically exceeding 1,000%. Prices typically fluctuate so quickly during hyperinflation that it becomes a daily occurrence, and money's value drops sharply.
